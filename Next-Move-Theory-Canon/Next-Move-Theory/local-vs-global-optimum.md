# Local vs Global optimum

## Definitions (mathematical metaphor)

- **The hill metaphor**: Local and global optimum --- these are terms from mathematics that describe and explain the decisions we make, the risks, and the benefit and payoff from those decisions very well. Picture this: you're walking across terrain and you see a hill in front of you. That hill is the only thing you see (flat steppe all around, and a tall hill in front of you). That hill is the **local optimum**. That is, you see that you can climb this hill, and you don't see any higher points.
- **Once you've climbed the hill**: You can see that beyond this hill there's actually a mountain range, and there are huge-huge-huge mountains there. But while you were standing in front of the hill, those mountains may have been invisible to you. The **global optimum** is something fundamentally different (maybe a different country, maybe an entirely different Job, maybe not just companies that sell something on a marketplace).

## Local optimum

- **Definition in business**: When you don't fundamentally change the segment, don't fundamentally change the Core Jobs, don't change the business model. You were selling SaaS for marketing optimization on a marketplace --- and you keep selling it. Yes, you do it a bit better, yes, a bit fewer problems, yes, a bit more efficiently, yes, a bit better efficiency for the customer, yes, you've added some adjacent segment --- but you don't fundamentally change the Core Jobs.
- **Characteristics**: Low-risk investments that by definition can give you less return on investment than the global optimum. Limited growth (+10–30%).
- **The local-optimum hypothesis zone**: This is the stuff where we can't lose much money. This is what on average gets delegated to **less competent employees**.
- **Examples**: A/B tests, UX improvements, conversion growth, feature work for A/B segments.
- **A task for Junior/Middle specialists**.
- Improving the current product in the current segment (A/B tests, features).
- Low risk, low growth ceiling.

### What you can do in the local optimum

- **Add +1 Job below the level**: Perform an additional Job in the graph.
- **Fix problems with the current solution**: Remove bugs, improve UX.
- **Better execution of the critical sequence**: Optimize the user's main path.
- **Kill a Job below the level**: Automate what used to be done manually --- it gets a bit more convenient.
- **Grow conversion and return rate**: Funnel optimization, retention.
- **Acquire a bit better**: Optimize channels and creatives.

### What does NOT change in the local optimum

- **Doesn't fundamentally change**: Segment, Core Jobs, business model.
- **Low-risk zone**: This is the stuff where we can't lose much money.
- **Delegation**: This is what on average gets delegated to less competent employees.

## Global optimum

- **Definition**: You can change the fundamental segment, Core Jobs, move to Previous Jobs, Next Jobs, add adjacent high-frequency Core Jobs, change the business model. You enter another country, or for example move up to a Job a level higher.
- **Characteristics**: A riskier investment, but by definition its **return on investment can be much larger**. High risk, potentially multiplicative growth (x2–x10).
- **Examples**: If you exit Russia for the international market --- a market tens of times larger opens up to you. If you change the business model (you used to sell home gym equipment, and now you sell it like Peloton --- equipment with a subscription) --- that business model in principle lets you manage margin across the customer's entire lifetime, lets you retain the customer, lets you upsell them.
- **A task for founders and C-level**.
- Changing the segment, business model, market, or Core Job.

### Examples of the global optimum

- **Country switch**: Exit from Russia to the international market --- a market tens of times larger.
- **Business-model switch**: You used to sell home gym equipment, now like Peloton (equipment with a subscription) --- lets you manage margin across the customer's entire lifetime, retain, upsell.
- **Segment switch**: Find and create value for segments outside your current paying customers (because your market share could be 0.1% --- yes, great, you can carve out segments among the customers you're already acquiring, but it's unlikely you have value and knowledge for how to acquire segments among the 99% of customers you don't yet have).
- **Move up to a Job a level higher**: Compete not for the Core Job, but for the Big Job.

## Balance and delegation

- **Delegating tasks by risk**: Top management --- launching new products, solving the hardest problems, the most important and risky partnerships. Juniors, mids, PMs --- get small zones of responsibility ("Here's your product card on a marketplace, your job is to grow conversion: to purchase, to wishlist, to writing reviews, and so on").
- **More competent --- more complex**: More competent employees get more complex, riskier tasks. Less competent --- less complex, less risky.
- **Optimize locally (for stability) and search globally (for growth) at the same time**: Balance.
- **Not an opposition**: Local and global aren't an opposition, they're two parallel investment tracks --- you can fund both simultaneously.

## Local-optimum strategies (concrete actions)

- **Segment existing customers**: ABCD segmentation, Sean Ellis segmentation.
- **For AB segments**: Create value from the Job tree toward the targets:
  - Fix problems with the current solution
  - Perform a +1 Job from the Job tree toward the targets
  - Kill one of the Jobs a level below
- **Grow conversion and return rate**.
- **Level up acquisition**: Including by performing the previous Job.

## Global-optimum strategies (concrete actions)

- **Switch the segment by Core Jobs/Big Jobs**: Find new segments outside current paying customers.
- **Switch the country**: Enter the international market.
- **Move up to a higher-level Job**: Compete for the Big Job instead of the Core Job.
- **Switch the business model**: Move to a more effective monetization model.

## The question of choosing between Local and Global Optima

- **The question**: From investments in Global Optima or Local Optima, which gives us a larger return on investment --- and over what time horizons?
- **Do we have employees competent enough?**: The global optimum requires more competent people.
- **What do shareholders/stakeholders expect from us?**: Expectations shape the acceptable risk level and the planning horizon.

## The Innovator's Dilemma

- **The Innovator's Dilemma**: Fear of losing current revenue prevents you from investing in the global optimum until competitors overtake you.
- **Studying the graph and applying strategic mechanics --- a way out of the Innovator's Dilemma**.
- Next Move Theory helps you make this jump deliberately.
- **When to switch**: If the local optimum is exhausted (growth has stalled, competition has intensified, the market has shrunk) --- it's time to look for the global one.
