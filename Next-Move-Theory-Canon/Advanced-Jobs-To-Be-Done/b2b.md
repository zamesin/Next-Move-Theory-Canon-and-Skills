# B2B specifics

This file is the canon page on **how B2B differs from B2C structurally** — how every layer of AJTBD (Jobs, Job Graph, Critical Chain, segmentation, value creation) bends when the buyer is a company. The single structural fact that organizes the page: **a B2B purchase is not one person performing one Job — it is several roles, each carrying two parallel Job Graphs (business + personal), running a multi-week multi-handoff Critical Chain that must complete end-to-end before any money moves.** How much of the page applies scales with deal size — SMB collapses most roles into one person; mid-market and enterprise separate them (§9).

The page sits on top of [Job Structure](job-structure.md) (the eight-element Job structure — used here per role, on two parallel graphs) and [Critical Chain](critical-chain.md) (the operational core; §10 holds the canonical B2B chain as *roles × Jobs-per-role*; §4 and §5 hold the per-role walk dynamics — drop-off as Solution switch, predictions, the break cascade). It feeds [Segmentation](segmentation.md), [Riskiest Assumption Test §2](../Riskiest-Assumption-Test/rat-key-theses.md), and [ABCDX Segmentation](../ABCDX-Segmentation/abcdx-segmentation-key-theses.md). B2B interview methodology lives in [AJTBD interview guide](../HowTos/basic-ajtbd-interview-guide-and-principles.md).

## 1. The B2B deal is structurally complex for every participant — and the complexity dominates every downstream decision

**A B2B deal is not a scaled-up B2C purchase — it is a categorically different structure.** Multiple roles (§7), two parallel Job Graphs per role (§5), multi-week duration, and a chain that can break at any role-to-role boundary. The structural complexity is the central fact every other thesis on this page is downstream of.

**The recurring patterns that anchor the rest of the methodology:**

- **Buyers spend a small share of decision time talking to any one vendor** — [Gartner's *B2B Buying Journey* research](https://www.gartner.com/en/sales/insights/b2b-buying-journey) puts it at ~17% of total purchase time across *all* suppliers combined, so any single vendor gets a sliver. Most of the deal happens outside the seller's view; the machinery you cannot watch is where the deal is actually decided.
- **B2B decision-makers often experience the buying process itself as difficult, slow, and politically risky.** Alongside evaluating value, the buyer is trying to survive the internal process of buying.
- **Deal length is structurally multi-week or multi-month, with a long tail for larger accounts.** A same-day B2C funnel is the wrong mental model for B2B.

Three consequences fall out at once. **Most of what determines whether you win happens outside the room with you** — stakeholders the seller never meets (IT, security, legal, finance, a former implementer of the incumbent) hold veto rights, and the seller's leverage is *equipping the champion with everything those stakeholders will ask before they ask it* (§3). **Plan acquisition and pricing motions against long buying cycles, not same-day conversion** — a B2C funnel optimized for immediate purchase is structurally wrong for B2B. **The deal's complexity tax is itself a force holding customers on the incumbent** — a B2B customer who paid the tax once won't pay it again unless the gain visibly exceeds it. Switching cost in B2B is not a feature gap; it is a multi-quarter operational disruption — a habit force ([Behaviour Change §9](behaviour-change.md)) scaled to the company level. The structural response is in §2.

**The default outcome of all this complexity is *no decision* — not a competitor win.** The dominant B2B competitor is the *null Solution* ([Critical Chain §4](critical-chain.md)): the buyer abandons the purchase and stays with what they already have, because the complexity tax plus the personal risk of a failed implementation (§5) makes *doing nothing* the safe choice. A large share of qualified B2B pipeline dies here, not in competitive loss — so the first job is rarely out-comparing rivals; it is making the cost of the status quo concrete and unavoidable enough that *doing nothing* stops being the safe option.

## 2. Product value doesn't always dominate — sales motion, relationships, and integrator position frequently win the deal

**Sometimes product value is the deciding input. Often it is not.** In B2B, product quality is one input among several, and the others — *whether the seller has a working relationship with the buyer*, *whether the buyer's chain ran cleanly the last time it ran*, *whether the seller can answer the questions the absent stakeholders will ask* — frequently dominate. Product quality compounds on top of those; it doesn't substitute for them.

**When product value alone does dominate:** novel categories where no incumbent exists; deeply technical buyers evaluating like engineers (developer-tools, infrastructure, security primitives); regulated environments where a specific compliance feature is non-negotiable; segments where the buyer's personal Job is itself *"pick the technically best option to prove my judgment"* (Personal Job 3 in §6). **When other forces dominate:** mature categories with established incumbents; risk-averse buyers (most enterprise mid-market); deals where switching cost is large relative to the value delta; categories where the analyst landscape and peer-recommendation channel are well-formed.

**§2 and §5 are two sides of one mechanism.** Relationships and integrator position outperform raw product quality precisely because they address what the product *alone* cannot — the Decision Maker's personal Jobs (§5): Job-security continuity, a named customer success manager (CSM), predictable account management, and a *"we made the safe choice with a known partner"* story. A better product with no relationship offers superior business value while exposing the Decision Maker to blame risk on every personal Job at once — so the buyer's brain, scoring the *combined* business + personal stack ([Value Creation §4](value-creation.md)), picks the relationship.

**The integrator model — the structural endgame.** Once a B2B vendor clears the first deal cycle with a customer, additional sales become *easier*, not harder: the integration is already wired, stakeholders already know the vendor, the salesforce already knows the buyer's roles and budget cycle, and *"we already have [vendor X] — what else can [vendor X] do?"* conversations become a free acquisition channel. This is the mechanism behind cross-sell and expansion revenue compounding faster than initial sales in mature B2B SaaS — visible in [Atlassian's land-with-Jira-then-expand-to-Confluence-and-Bitbucket](https://www.atlassian.com) playbook and in HubSpot's evolution from marketing-automation single-product to a six-Hub platform.

## 3. Information that simplifies the deal is one of the highest-leverage acquisition assets — bring it pre-loaded for every absent stakeholder

**Sellers who provide buyers with useful information on alternatives, objection handling, and stakeholder questions close materially more often — and the deal itself runs materially simpler.** This falls out directly from §1: most of the deal happens outside the seller's view, so the seller's leverage is *what they put in the champion's hands before the champion talks to the absent stakeholders.*

**The catalog of information that actually moves deals:**

- **Side-by-side comparison with the named competitors the buyer is evaluating** — not generic claims of superiority. *"Here is how we differ from Stripe Connect, Adyen, and Worldpay on the dimensions your finance team will ask about."*
- **Stakeholder-specific objection responses** — *"Your IT director will ask about SOC 2 attestation, single-tenant deployment options, and a service-level agreement (SLA). Here is the one-page answer for each."*
- **Customer references in the buyer's specific industry and size band** — *"Three logos in healthcare-payer SaaS at your scale bought this product in the last 18 months. Here are the implementation timelines."*
- **A pre-built internal business case template** — the champion copies, edits, and forwards to the Approver without constructing the financial argument from scratch.
- **A migration / rollout playbook** — proof that the implementation chain (§1's "very complex" finding) has been run before and converges.

Buyer enablement is the most under-invested high-leverage budget line in most B2B marketing teams — most budget goes to demand generation (top of funnel), while the largest leverage often sits at the conversion gate. But this is *capture*, not *creation*: only ~5% of a B2B market is in-market at any moment (the [95-5 rule](https://inversion.agency/articles/rule-955) — Ehrenberg-Bass / LinkedIn B2B Institute), so bottom-of-funnel enablement wins the buyer who is *already* deciding, while brand and mental-availability work is what lands you in the Consideration Set of the 95% who will buy later — the two do different jobs, and a healthy B2B mix funds both. The enablement layer itself — [Stripe's docs](https://stripe.com/docs), [Notion's templates gallery](https://www.notion.com/templates), [HubSpot Academy](https://academy.hubspot.com) — compounds across multi-touch sales cycles. **A "deal room" — a single shared URL containing every artifact** (comparison sheets, stakeholder-specific FAQs, references, business case, rollout playbook) — is increasingly the operational form of this thesis. Vendors who give the champion one URL outperform vendors who scatter the same content across PDFs and email threads.

## 4. Consideration Activators in B2B flows through peer and institutional channels — not consumer-marketing channels

**The channels that wake up a B2B Job are categorically different from B2C.** A consumer evaluating a personal-finance app responds to landing pages, social ads, influencer recommendations. A B2B Decision Maker evaluating an enterprise platform doesn't — they respond to *peer stories from competitors, customer-published case studies, conference talks, analytical reports, and what trusted colleagues recommend.* The behaviour-change mechanism is identical to B2C ([Behaviour Change §5](behaviour-change.md)) — but the Consideration Activators channels that load Jobs into the Decision Maker's head are channels the Decision Maker trusts as professional-grade signal.

**The B2B Consideration Activators channel stack, roughly ordered by per-touch impact:**

- **Peer stories from companies in the buyer's reference group.** A CTO at a Series B SaaS company is told by a CTO friend at a peer Series B company that their team moved from X to Y last quarter, what worked, what didn't. Highest-impact channel by a wide margin — carries both information (what does the product actually do?) and trust (the peer chose it; if they were burned they'd say so).
- **Customer case studies, especially in blog form.** The buyer's preferred form is rarely the polished PDF case study — it is a long-form blog post by the customer's own engineering / product / ops team explaining what they actually built, what surprised them, what they would do differently. [Stripe's customer stories](https://stripe.com/customers), [Notion's customer pages](https://www.notion.so/customers), engineering blogs at customer companies that mention the vendor by name — all carry materially more weight than vendor-produced marketing.
- **Conference talks by customers, not by the vendor.** A talk titled *"How we migrated 50M users from legacy auth to [vendor]"* delivered by the customer's senior engineer at a category conference loads Consideration Activators at peer-credible weight; the vendor's keynote at the same conference loads Consideration Activators at vendor-marketing weight. The customer-told version is meaningfully harder to engineer; it converts better when it lands.
- **Analytical and industry reports.** *Industry reports of any source the buyer's procurement function accepts as third-party validation.* In some categories that's [Gartner](https://www.gartner.com) / [Forrester](https://www.forrester.com); in others it's IDC, McKinsey vertical-practice reports, Crunchbase market maps, or the buyer's industry trade association. The function is the same: third-party signal that the vendor is "real."
- **Trusted-colleague recommendation inside the buyer's own company.** A senior engineer the Decision Maker trusts says *"I'd pick this one."* Lowest cost per touch; highest information-density per touch; impossible to engineer directly. The vendor's lever: get the senior engineer to evaluate the product first.
- **Vendor-produced content (docs, blog posts, technical deep-dives).** Doesn't substitute for peer / customer / analyst channels — but is the substrate the peer-channel signals point back to. The buyer's engineer who hears the peer-recommendation googles the vendor, hits the docs, and either confirms or falsifies the peer story. Strong docs make the peer signal stick; weak docs unwind it.

Most B2B marketing budget over-invests in vendor-produced content and under-invests in peer-channel enablement. The vendor can't write peer recommendations directly — but can make them measurably easier: customer-success teams whose explicit Job is to surface customer stories, named-customer reference programs, customer-advisory boards, talk-coaching for customer engineers, sponsored editorial in industry publications customers actually read. **Cold acquisition in B2B is mostly cold Consideration Activators-loading** — inbound paid campaigns work only when there's already a warm channel feeding them (peer talk, an analyst's report, a customer's blog post the prospect found). Cold paid spend without warm Consideration Activators behind it converts weakly in most B2B categories — and against the 95% not yet in-market (§3), its realistic job is building mental availability for a later trigger, not immediate conversion.

## 5. B2B motivation is mostly personal Jobs, not business Jobs — every role carries two parallel Job Graphs

**The single most counter-intuitive thesis on this page.** Default product-team intuition: B2B decisions are made *as professionals*, on the merits of the business case, with KPI achievement as the primary driver. Reality reported across multiple B2B research engagements at mid-market and enterprise scale: **personal Jobs *gate* the decision and business Jobs *justify* it.** The business case is necessary — it gets the deal on the table and past finance — but the human in the seat moves on Jobs they perform on their own behalf, with the business KPI as the *frame* the personal Job runs inside.  The exact split varies by industry, deal size, role, and founder involvement; the structural point is that the personal Jobs usually decide whether the human moves at all.

**Every role in a B2B deal carries two parallel Job Graphs simultaneously.**

- **Business Job Graph.** What the role's KPIs are — *hit the sales plan with fewer FTEs; reduce defect rate by 30%; cut customer-support cost by 20%; ship the platform migration by end of Q3.* Visible, written down, evaluated in performance reviews.
- **Personal Job Graph.** What the human in the role performs for themselves — *don't get fired; get promoted; speak at a conference; not look incompetent in front of the CEO; not be left holding a failed implementation.* Unspoken, rarely written down, usually invisible to vendors who haven't asked.

**Two contexts that vary the business/personal ratio:**

- **Active founders + Decision Maker = CEO or CEO-minus-one → ~50/50.** When the company's founders are still active in operations and the Decision Maker sits one or zero levels below them, business motivation is materially stronger because the Decision Maker is being evaluated by people who care about business outcomes directly.
- **Decision Maker is two-or-more levels below the CEO, OR founders / owners no longer active → personal Jobs usually dominate.** The default in most US mid-market and enterprise organizations.

[John Maynard Keynes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Maynard_Keynes), paraphrased from *The General Theory of Employment, Interest, and Money* (1936): *"The principal personal task of the employee in a company is to reduce the risk of being blamed."* Keynes wrote about labor markets in general; the framing maps directly: B2B decisions are dominated by blame-avoidance, and the seller who reduces blame risk for the Decision Maker dominates the seller who maximizes business value.

Communication, demos, and sales scripts must hit personal Jobs explicitly. A landing page that names *"sleep at night knowing the audit log is complete"* is hitting a personal Job; a page that names *"reduce filing errors"* is hitting only the business Job. The product itself should be designed against personal Jobs alongside business Jobs — a product that hits the business KPI but exposes the Decision Maker to blame risk (incomplete audit logs, weak SSO, no rollback path) will lose to a product that hits the KPI marginally less well *and* gives the Decision Maker a defensible "I picked the safe option" narrative.

**Surfacing the personal Job in interview — climb up from the business Core Job, don't ask for it directly.** Direct *"what are your personal motivations for this?"* almost never works — the respondent has no ready language for the question and answers with the business Job by default. The reliable move is to **anchor on the business Core Job the role already named, then climb one rung up into the personal graph** with a single question:

> ***"What personal result, as a human, will you get from {Expected Outcome of business Core Job}?"***

Anchored to the business Job they're already explaining, it lets the respondent ladder up from familiar ground into the unspoken layer (*"I'll get a promotion," "I won't get fired," "I'll get to tell that story at the next conference," "I'll finally feel competent in my role."*) — usually a sentence they've never said out loud before. Each answer is a *single rung*; repeat the question against each surfaced personal Job (*"and what result do you get from getting promoted?"*) to climb toward the role-holder's life-level needs (status, safety, autonomy, competence). Stop one rung below pure needs — those are too abstract to design against ([Job Structure §1](job-structure.md)). Interview discipline lives in [Job Structure §16](job-structure.md) and [AJTBD interview guide](../HowTos/basic-ajtbd-interview-guide-and-principles.md).

## 6. The five personal Jobs of every B2B Decision Maker

**Across the consulted-expert corpus and Ivan's agency research, B2B Decision Maker personal Jobs cluster into five recurring patterns.** Treat as a starter checklist to validate per segment, not a universal law — but the five Jobs below appear in some form in the majority of mid-market and enterprise B2B deals studied. They are *Decision-Maker*-flavored; every other role in the chain (§7) carries its own personal-Job profile — the CFO's, the IT director's, and the champion's differ sharply.

- **Personal Job 1 — *"I want to make a choice that won't get me blamed or fired"*** (Job security; colloquial form: *"no one will fire me"*). The default dominant personal Job. The Decision Maker is choosing a solution whose downside they will be personally accountable for; the worst case is being publicly responsible for an expensive failed implementation. *Communication:* "Our solution is safe — your KPI will be hit, with no surprises you will have to explain afterward." *Operational instruments:* SOC 2 / ISO 27001 attestations, named enterprise customers in the same industry, money-back or pilot-period clauses, named implementation manager and CSM, transparent SLA. **The single most under-invested personal Job for product teams scaling from SMB to mid-market** — SMB buyers operate without it; mid-market and enterprise buyers operate with it as dominant.
- **Personal Job 2 — *"I want to use the same tools the best people in my field use"*** (status; colloquial: *"I'll be with the best"*). The Decision Maker wants to operate at the level of *the best people in my field with the best tools.* Buying a status-loaded product is itself a status-uplift act. *Communication:* "The leaders in your space already use this — here are the logos." *Operational instrument:* customer-logo wall — and the reason it almost always works disproportionately to its information content. What matters is *which* logos sit on it, not how many.
- **Personal Job 3 — *"I want to build a career-defining story from this implementation"*** (career upside; colloquial: *"my position in the company will grow"*). The Decision Maker is performing the business Job partly as a vehicle for their own advancement. A successful implementation produces a story the Decision Maker can tell in performance reviews, conference talks, the next interview. An expensive product is often a stronger fit for this Job than a cheap one — the price tag itself signals importance and seriousness, and the Decision Maker gets to tell a *"we made the right call; we picked the premium solution"* story. *Communication:* "You'll be telling your CEO the story of how this implementation moved the business. Here is the template."
- **Personal Job 4 — *"I want to offload operational routine in order to focus on strategic work"*** (escape from drudgery; colloquial: *"I don't want to do boring routine"*). A significant share of any Decision Maker's work is administrative routine — running reports, filling tickets, attending stand-ups about backlog grooming. The Decision Maker wants to be working on strategic problems, talking at conferences, leading visible initiatives. *Communication:* "We take A, B, C, D off your plate so you can spend your week on work that matters." *Operational instruments:* workflow automation, AI-driven defaults, set-and-forget configuration, escalation paths that don't require Decision Maker attention by default. The HR-function pattern — HR teams accumulate operational task dumps from every other department; products like [Workday](https://www.workday.com) and [Rippling](https://www.rippling.com) win HR by performing this Job at scale.
- **Personal Job 5 — *"I want to pick a vendor who will still support me at year 3"*** (orphan-implementer avoidance; colloquial: *"I won't be left without support"*). The Decision Maker has been burned before — buying a solution, justifying the spend, deploying it, then having the vendor disappear, raise prices unexpectedly, or fail to support a critical integration mid-rollout. *Communication:* "You will have a named CSM, response SLA of X hours, escalation path Y, and our roadmap is public for the next four quarters." *Operational instruments:* published roadmap, named-rep contact, status-page transparency, written escalation procedures, customer-advisory-board membership. **This is also why "we just raised $50M Series B" lands as a positive signal in enterprise sales** — it reduces vendor-disappearance risk.

**The empirical lift.** The [CEB / Google *From Promotion to Emotion* B2B study](https://www.marketingcharts.com/industries/business-to-business-37036) (~3,000 B2B buyers) found that buyers who perceive personal value are far more likely to purchase, and to pay a premium, than those who see only business value. Per-segment effects vary; the durable finding is the direction and size of the lift, not a universal multiplier.

## 7. A B2B deal is a chain of roles, each carrying veto or veto-equivalent rights

**A B2B deal involves not one decision-maker but a chain of roles, each carrying its own pair of Job Graphs (§5) and veto or veto-equivalent rights.** SMB collapses several roles into one person; mid-market and enterprise separate them. Mapping the role chain — and the personal Jobs of every role in it — is the prerequisite for any sales motion that scales. The role names below follow the MEDDIC / MEDDPICC vocabulary standard in US enterprise sales, so most sales orgs will recognize them.

### The eight roles

- **Executive Sponsor (Business Sponsor) — the role that *gets the value*.** The senior leader whose KPIs improve when the product is bought. *Examples:* a VP of Sales whose plan calls for $50M in annual recurring revenue with the same headcount; a Head of Customer Support whose mandate is *"reduce ticket volume 30% without growing the team."* Their business Job is the visible motivation; their personal Job is usually some flavor of "demonstrate I delivered against this mandate." **Necessary but not sufficient** — they rarely have budget authority alone and don't carry the technical or compliance veto rights. In some MEDDIC-aligned orgs this role and the Economic Buyer below are the same person; in others they are separated.
- **Economic Buyer (Decision Maker) — the most expensive person on the call, with the final word.** The role with formal decision authority over this category — often the CEO in startups, the COO or Head of [function] in mid-market, the SVP / EVP in enterprise. Takes the recommendation from the Executive Sponsor, runs it through absent stakeholders' filters, and either approves or blocks. *MEDDIC: Economic Buyer; everyday usage: Decision Maker.* **Identifying the actual Economic Buyer (not the role that *looks* like the Decision Maker on the org chart) is one of the highest-leverage moves in B2B research** — a recurring failure mode is interviewing someone called the "Decision Maker" who turns out to be the Executive Sponsor or Champion rather than the budget-signoff authority.
- **Approver (Finance / Budget gate) — the formal budget-signoff role, usually the CFO.** In SMB and flat orgs, the Economic Buyer and the Approver are the same person; in mid-market and enterprise they are separated to reduce fraud, balance competing departmental wants, prevent impulse buys. The Approver typically doesn't engage with the product but reviews the business case. **Their personal Job is almost always *"don't approve a spend that will look stupid in the year-end review"*** — arm the Economic Buyer with an Approver-ready one-page business case *before* the Economic Buyer walks into that conversation.
- **Technical Buyer — IT, Security, DevOps (the technical-fit gate).** The role that judges technical fit. *Examples:* the IT director who has to take the new system into their perimeter and support it operationally; the security officer who reviews data handling, encryption, access controls, breach risk; the DevOps lead who evaluates integration complexity and operational load. **In enterprise deals, Technical Buyer veto is the single most common reason mid-market SaaS deals stall above ~$50K in annual contract value (ACV)** — typically blocking on missing SOC 2 attestation, SSO/SAML/SCIM, audit log, or role-based admin. Notion Enterprise (2021) launched the SSO/SAML/SCIM/audit-log bundle specifically to unblock enterprise Technical Buyer veto.
- **Gatekeeper — Procurement, Legal, Compliance (the process-and-risk gate).** Roles that judge contract terms, vendor-risk standards, procurement procedure. *Examples:* procurement runs the vendor through the standard request-for-proposal, request-for-information, and master-service-agreement cycle; legal reviews indemnification, intellectual-property, and data-residency clauses; compliance reviews regulatory fit (HIPAA, FERPA, SOX, GDPR / CCPA). Gatekeepers have process-veto rights — they don't decide whether to buy, but they decide whether the deal can move through procurement at all. Zoom for Education (2020) launched FERPA-compliant configurations specifically to clear K-12 procurement-Gatekeeper veto.
- **Coach — internal advisor who provides intel and political guidance.** *A MEDDIC term, distinct from Champion below.* A person inside the buying org who informally shares context — *"the real Economic Buyer is the COO, not the VP";* *"your real competitor is the in-house team, not the vendor on the shortlist";* *"the Approver hates 3-year contracts."* Coaches don't actively push the deal; they reveal the chain. *(In looser usage often called "Advisor" or "Recommender"; analysts like [Gartner](https://www.gartner.com) and [Forrester](https://www.forrester.com) play the external-Coach role for the buyer.)*
- **Champion — the role that puts in active effort to *get the deal done*.** The most active form of internal advocate. People whose personal Jobs are *served by the deal succeeding* — usually because the product hits the career-upside personal Job (Personal Job 3) or the escape-from-routine personal Job (Personal Job 4). Champions do work the seller never asked for: calendar internal meetings, draft business cases, escalate to the Economic Buyer, push back against Technical-Buyer veto. **A B2B deal at any meaningful complexity is dramatically harder to close without a Champion** — without one, deals stall while no one inside the buyer's organization spends political capital to move them. But not every willing advocate can move the committee: a friendly *Talker* who can't drive internal consensus is the most common silent-kill — recruit the Champion who actually mobilizes the group (the *Mobilizer*), not the one who is merely warm. *MEDDPICC: the "C" in MEDDPICC is Champion.*
- **Detractor (Anti-Champion / Saboteur) — covert opposer with personal reasons to block.** The mirror of Champion. People whose personal Jobs are *served by the deal failing.* *Examples:* the Economic Buyer behind a previous implementation of the incumbent — if your product wins, their prior win is publicly devalued; the head of an internal team building a competing in-house solution; the executive whose department headcount shrinks if your product automates work currently done by their team. **Detractors are usually invisible from outside the buying organization and rarely surface in the first interview** — surface them with questions like *"who might be against this deal? who could block it? who blocked similar deals in the past?"* on the second or third pass. *(Industry terminology varies — "Detractor" and "Anti-Champion" are common in US sales-training contexts; "Saboteur" is also used.)*
- **User Buyer (End User) — the people who use the product daily.** The people whose work changes when the product is deployed. The role the product's UX is built for in B2C — and the role with the *least* purchase weight in B2B (§8 unpacks the exception). User Buyers feed business value into the Executive Sponsor by using the product; their Jobs matter for activation, retention, and seat-expansion — but rarely for the initial purchase decision.

## 8. End-user UX is a purchase input when the role is scarce — or when adoption is product-led

**The default in B2B is that end-user Jobs don't influence the purchase decision** — IT, security, finance, and the Decision Maker hold the veto rights; the end user is the *output* the product serves, not the *input* to the decision. Two structural exceptions break the default. **The first — labor leverage: when the end-user role is itself a scarce, expensive, hard-to-retain hire, their tooling preferences become a purchase input.** **The second — adoption leverage: when the motion is bottom-up / product-led, end-users adopt the product themselves and *become* the buying signal** (product-qualified leads), so end-user UX *is* the acquisition engine regardless of how replaceable the role is. The two often compound (Cursor and Figma below run on both); this section unpacks the labor-market case in depth.

**The *ex* rule** : *if a role, like an ex, is hard to find, easy to lose, and impossible to forget — they carry purchase weight.* Senior software engineers, senior designers, senior data scientists, top sales reps — roles whose departure is itself a business event — get tooling preferences honored. *"I won't work here if you make me use Confluence instead of Notion"* is a credible labor threat when the labor market for that role favors the employee; the company gives ground because the cost of replacing the engineer dominates the cost of the SaaS subscription.

**The dynamic flips when the labor market favors the employer.** Bookkeepers, customer-support agents, operations associates — roles where the labor pool is deep and the employer dictates terms — get whatever tooling the company picks, and their UX preferences don't move the purchase. This is why mass-market B2B operations software ([NetSuite](https://www.netsuite.com), [SAP Business One](https://www.sap.com/products/erp/business-one.html), most legacy ERP) ships with notoriously rough end-user UX — *the buying decision was made above the end user, against the company's KPI, not against the end user's preference.*

**Two contemporary US patterns running on this dynamic:**

- **Cursor's rapid displacement of legacy IDEs (~2023–2025).** Senior engineers — currently the scarcest hire in tech — chose Cursor's AI-native editor over [JetBrains](https://www.jetbrains.com) and VS Code defaults; companies switched because retaining the engineer dominated the licensing cost. The buying motion is bottom-up, driven entirely by end-user preference at a role where the labor market favors the employee.
- **The Figma displacement of Sketch + Adobe XD (~2018–2023).** Designers were the scarce hire; designers preferred Figma's collaboration model; companies switched because designers threatened to leave. Adobe ultimately discontinued XD in 2023 after failing to compete on the engineer/designer-driven adoption motion.

Categorize your end-user segment by labor-market dynamics before designing the UX investment. If your end user is an in-demand engineer, designer, or data-science role — invest heavily in end-user UX as a *purchase input.* If your end user is a back-office or operational role — invest minimally in end-user UX *for the purchase decision*, then re-evaluate for retention and activation downstream. **B2B end-user UX is consistently worse than B2C UX, and the gap is not laziness — it is a rational allocation against the actual buyer.** Treating this as a problem-to-solve for *all* B2B segments mis-allocates engineering investment; treat it as a problem-to-solve for segments where the end-user role is scarce — or where the motion is product-led and the end user is the buying signal.

## 9. The Critical Chain in B2B — roles × Jobs-per-role, breaks at role boundaries, personal-Job failures dominant

**A B2B Critical Chain is not a linear sequence of Jobs the customer walks** ([Critical Chain §1](critical-chain.md) for the B2C base case) — **it is a *roles × Jobs-per-role* matrix** ([Critical Chain §10](critical-chain.md)), with hand-offs at every role boundary and two parallel Job Graphs per role. The structural property: *no money moves until every role's business Jobs and personal Jobs at every step are performed.* These per-role Jobs map onto the six non-linear *buying jobs* [Gartner](https://www.gartner.com/en/sales/insights/b2b-buying-journey) names — problem identification, solution exploration, requirements building, supplier selection, validation, and consensus creation — which the committee loops through rather than completes in order.

**Where the chain breaks** (canonical patterns from [Critical Chain §10](critical-chain.md), refined per the B2B specifics on this page):

- **IT security review.** Champion wants the tool, Executive Sponsor approves intent, Decision Maker signals go-ahead — and IT blocks on missing SOC 2, SSO/SAML, audit log, or role-based admin. The chain breaks at *Executive Sponsor → Technical Buyer* — the $50K-ACV stall point (§7).
- **Clinical / regulated-workflow integration.** CMO approves, IT integrates, clinicians (or analysts, or operators) refuse because the new tool doesn't write back to the system of record (Epic, Salesforce, NetSuite). The Tax Job the buyer never saw at evaluation time becomes the chain-break at rollout.
- **B2B2C marketplaces.** Payment platform + marketplace + merchant + consumer. The break at marketplace ↔ merchant onboarding shows up as a consumer-side metric drop weeks later.
- **Procurement / legal late-stage stalls.** Deal closed verbally; legal then refuses to sign on indemnification language or data-residency clauses; the deal slips a quarter or dies.

**Most B2B chain-breaks are *personal-Job* failures at role boundaries, not business-Job failures** — the §5 personal-Job dominance applied to role boundaries. The IT director's *business Job* (*"keep the systems running"*) may be served by your product; their *personal Job* (*"don't let an un-audited tool into my perimeter — if it leaks, I'm fired"*) fires the veto, and an airtight business case still dies at IT review when the seller never addressed it.

**Chain length varies sharply by deal size:**

- **SMB B2B** — 1–2 roles (founder = Executive Sponsor = Decision Maker = Approver; or founder + one IT-savvy operator). Chain breaks are rare; deals close in days or weeks.
- **Mid-market** — 3–5 roles (Executive Sponsor, Decision Maker, Approver / CFO, IT, sometimes legal). Most deals; most chain breaks; most leverage from the §3 information-asset thesis.
- **Enterprise** — 7+ roles (champion, Decision Maker, IT, security, legal, procurement, end-user representatives, sometimes a sponsoring executive). Chain breaks are the default state; expect 3–6+ month cycles; the §3 thesis becomes operationally mandatory.

Map the B2B Critical Chain as *roles × Jobs per role*, not as a linear path. Fix chain-breaks with role-specific evidence and tooling, not better core product — IT directors need SOC 2 attestation and a one-pager their CFO will accept, not a new product feature; legal needs a master-service-agreement template and indemnification language, not a price cut. Time outreach to predictable chain-receptivity windows ([Critical Chain §6](critical-chain.md)) — Q4 budget cycles, post-incident vendor reviews, new-role onboarding for the Decision Maker, regulatory-deadline windows. The receptivity bar is much lower during these windows than in steady state.

## 10. Segmentation in B2B — same Core-Jobs-and-criteria root as B2C, with three operational additions

**Segmentation in B2B is fundamentally the same as B2C** — sets of customers performing similar Core Jobs with similar success criteria (per [Segmentation](segmentation.md)). Industry, company size, and revenue band are *refinements*; Core Jobs and criteria are the root.

**Three operational additions for B2B that B2C doesn't have:**

- **Map the role chain per segment** (§7). Two segments with identical Core Jobs but different role chains (e.g., founder-led 50-person startup vs. functionally-split 5,000-person enterprise) require different sales motions even if the product is identical — different stakeholder maps, different objection handling, different sales-cycle length.
- **Map the deal-approval process per segment.** Bottom-up vs. top-down purchasing, formal procurement vs. credit-card swipe, proposal-required vs. purchase-order-issued — each shape needs a different funnel. [Slack's bottom-up growth into enterprises](https://www.slack.com) and [Salesforce's top-down enterprise sale](https://www.salesforce.com) are the two endpoint patterns; most B2B segments cluster around one or the other.
- **Map the budget cycle and budget-holder per segment.** A segment whose budget cycle is calendar-Q4 needs Q3 acquisition motion; a segment whose budget is project-based and approved ad-hoc needs always-on motion. The same product sold to two segments with different budget structures can look identical at acquisition and diverge sharply after renewal and expansion.

**B2B has lower Job-existence uncertainty than B2C** — a useful asymmetry for RAT prioritization. In B2C there is significant uncertainty whether the Big Job exists in the segment at all (dormant Jobs, novel categories, behavior-change required to activate the Job; see [Behaviour Change §5](behaviour-change.md) for Consideration Activators load on dormant Jobs and [Critical Chain §6](critical-chain.md) for the First-time vs. Nth-time path split). The main RAT risk is *is there demand?* In B2B, much lower uncertainty whether the Job exists — at any given moment a company is either already performing the Job (with the incumbent solution) or actively searching for a vendor against known criteria. The main RAT risk is *can we push our solution through the complexity tax (§1)? Do we know the role chain, the budget, the objections?*

For B2B research, build the Job hypothesis list before the first interview and use each interview to confirm-or-falsify rather than to discover from scratch. **Recruit the Decision Maker directly, not via an end-user**, even when the end-user is easier to find — end-user interviews surface end-user Jobs (often weak signal for purchase); Decision Maker interviews surface roles, deal process, budget, and objections (high signal for purchase). Focus interview budget on Problems with the incumbent, not on hypothetical features — a customer fails to perform a Job for only two reasons: it is *too hard* (a Barrier — [Barrier Removal](barrier-removal.md)) or *not important enough* (filtered upstream by past payment — [AJTBD key theses §15](ajtbd-key-theses.md)). In B2B, "not-important-enough" is filtered upstream by past payment; "hard" is what kills deals — and the source of "hard" is almost always a Problem with the incumbent that the new vendor can solve.

**ABCDX-on-B2B is a separate move** (per [ABCDX Segmentation](../ABCDX-Segmentation/abcdx-segmentation-key-theses.md)). Once a B2B base is paying, the per-account margin × satisfaction segmentation produces patterns that often invert team intuition. A recurring discovery shape in boutique B2B-services and SaaS practices: the team assumes the A-segment is mid-market customers in the $100–200K ACV band; ABCDX analysis surfaces that A is actually the top 10% by margin — a different industry, a different deal-size band, or a different use-case the product was delivering as a side-effect. A deliberate refocus on the actual-A segment routinely produces material revenue lift over the next few quarters. The general principle: ABCDX-on-A/B routinely identifies a segment within the paying base the team didn't realize was the focus segment.

## 11. B2B Job structure — same eight elements as B2C, with two new dimensions inside context

**The eight-element structure of a single Job** ([Job Structure §2–§10](job-structure.md)) — context, negative emotions, Consideration Set, trigger, expected outcome, success criteria, higher-level Job, positive emotions — **applies in B2B without modification.** What changes is *what sits inside the context element* — two new dimensions B2C doesn't have:

- **The company's past experience.** The company-level history that shapes how the current buyer evaluates options. *Example:* the company missed the mobile transition in 2009–2012 and watched competitors capture market share; now every new technology category (AI, voice, AR) gets pre-emptively evaluated to avoid repeating the miss. *Operational consequence:* the prior trauma is itself a lever for loading Consideration Activators — surface it in interviews (*"what category did your company miss in the past, and what did it cost?"*) and load against the trauma's current shape.
- **The company's structural traits.** Hierarchy depth, decision-making centralization, control-vs-trust culture, founder-led vs. professional-management, public vs. private. *Examples:* a 30,000-person bank requires every vendor through a 90-day procurement process; a 50-person Series A startup runs the entire vendor evaluation on Slack in a week. Same product, two utterly different deal structures — and the difference shows up before the first Personal Job is even surfaced.

**The Business Job's higher-level Job is the KPI written into the role's plan** — and at the top of the business chain, the KPI is itself terminal (no higher-level Job above it inside the company; above it sits the role-holder's personal Jobs, on the parallel graph). *"Hit the sales plan"* is terminal for the VP of Sales as a business Job; above it on the personal graph sits *"get promoted to CRO,"* *"not get fired,"* *"have a story to tell."* The two graphs are independent but interact at every node.

A complete B2B Job record contains the eight elements per role *twice* — once for the business graph, once for the personal graph. Records that only document the business graph are systematically incomplete, and the missing personal-graph side is exactly where the deal stalls. Cross-link the two graphs explicitly: the personal Job at each node is performed *in service of* the business Job at that node; the business Job is performed *because* the personal Job is served by performing it. Capture company traits and company past-experience at the segment level, not just the account level — company traits cluster by industry × size × maturity; mapping them once at the segment level lets the same trait map apply across all accounts in the segment.

## 12. Recruiting Decision Makers is structurally a sales activity, not a research activity — account-based marketing as the operational shape

**The hardest question in B2B research is not *what to ask* — it is *how to get the Decision Maker on a call at all.*** Decision Makers are scarce, time-poor, and structurally selective about whom they meet. The B2C recruitment shape (post on a panel, screen for criteria, schedule) does not scale to B2B; the operative shape is *treat recruitment as the first stage of sales* and build a multi-touch pipeline that converts contact into conversation.

**The reframe: you are not recruiting respondents — you are recruiting potential customers.** Every Decision Maker you put on a research call is also a sales prospect; every interview is both data and demand-generation. This recasts the cost-benefit of recruiting investment — *spending two weeks to land a single 45-minute Decision Maker call* is unreasonable as research budget allocation but entirely reasonable as account-based sales investment, and the call produces both deliverables. Mature research practices commonly decline engagements where the Decision Maker population is small enough (sub-1,000 globally) that the project is structurally sales-and-relationship-building rather than research; in those engagements the right investment is in business-development and account-executive capacity, not researcher capacity.

**The mechanics that work for cold Decision Maker recruitment** (rough order of conversion strength):

- **Warm intros, especially with a calendar handoff.** Someone the Decision Maker trusts forwards an intro and offers to set up the call. The highest-converting channel; multiplies all later channels.
- **Value-first outreach.** Write to the Decision Maker as *Director / VP / Head of X*, not as *researcher*, and lead with *value*, not a *favor*: name the likely Job, the trigger and the Problem with the current way, the value you deliver, then a soft ask. Re-positioning the call from *help me with research* to *here's value you can use* materially raises response rate.
- **Reference to people already interviewed.** *"I spoke with [recognizable name at peer company] last week — they recommended I talk with you about [the Job]."* Both a warmth signal and a peer-pressure signal.
- **Always ask for two intros at the end of every interview** — peer companies on the same Job, or the next role downstream in the chain. Compounding the network is the cheapest acquisition channel for the next round.
- **Webinar funnels and the offline-conference circuit.** Run a topic-led webinar; track which Decision Makers register, attend, and ask questions. Conferences: sales reps pre-book fifteen-minute slots with target accounts the day-of, exchange cards, follow up the next week — a two-day conference is a concentrated batch of net-new warm Decision Maker contacts.
- **Value-add offers in the intro.** *"We've just finished a study of 30 companies on [this Job] — happy to share the read in exchange for 30 minutes."* The Decision Maker trades time for asymmetric value, which lifts conversion materially.
- **Cold LinkedIn in US / Western Europe.** Cold LinkedIn outreach converts at meaningful rates when the message is well-written and lands a specific peer reference. Cold outreach conversion varies materially by region and category; calibrate the channel mix by market.

**The account-based marketing (ABM) algorithm** (rough seven-step structure standard across mature B2B-sales practices):

1. Compile the target-company list against the focus segment's ideal customer profile.
2. Parse open sources (LinkedIn, Crunchbase, company sites, press releases, podcast appearances) to identify the named Decision Maker at each company.
3. Enrich contacts across social channels — LinkedIn, X / Twitter, public Slack communities, conference speaker rosters, podcast guest lists.
4. Run topical webinars; track registration, attendance, Q&A engagement per account.
5. Send peer-referenced cold outreach with value-add offers; warm intros where available.
6. Conference attendance with pre-booked 15-minute slots.
7. Continuous list-enrichment as each interview produces 1–2 new peer intros.

A 2–3 person team can run this end-to-end; scale beyond that requires dedicated sales-development capacity. The activity *is* sales-and-marketing, executed under a research label — the boundary between research, demand-gen, and pipeline-building is intentionally blurred. Don't budget Decision Maker recruitment as a research cost line; budget it as sales / marketing investment with research as a deliverable. The team that does B2B research well is structurally cross-functional — a researcher, a sales-development rep, and a content-marketing partner working as one pod.

## 13. The B2B sale doesn't end at the signed contract — a significant share of "won" deals don't make it to renewal

**The B2B sale's real deliverable is the renewal in year 2, not the signature in year 1.** A first-year contract that doesn't renew often doesn't pay back — you spent the cost to win the customer and to implement them against a single year of revenue, so the deal can end underwater. It isn't always a loss: sometimes year one alone clears its cost, sometimes the customer comes back later. But non-renewal is the weakest version of a "win," and usually an avoidable one. Many "won" deals don't renew — not because a competitor took them, but because the work *after* signing broke down. How many churn varies by category, deal size, and economics; smaller customers churn more than enterprise. Whether the base grows or leaks over time is measured by net revenue retention — the signal that the integrator model (§2) is working; the full unit-economics treatment belongs to a future canon page.

**The post-sale chain has its own role chain and chain-breaks** — distinct from the pre-sale chain in §7 and §9, but structurally similar:

- **Onboarding.** Implementation manager + champion + end users; chain breaks when implementation drags past the buyer's expected timeline, the champion gets reassigned, or the end users can't get to first value within the activation window.
- **Adoption.** Champion + end users + occasional Decision Maker review; chain breaks when usage doesn't reach the activation threshold the business case was built on, the Decision Maker doesn't see the metric move, or end-user resistance accumulates.
- **Expansion.** Champion + new Executive Sponsor in adjacent function + Decision Maker; chain breaks when the next Job in the chain (per [Critical Chain §9](critical-chain.md)) doesn't materialize, or when an adjacent stakeholder vetoes the cross-function spread.
- **Renewal.** Decision Maker + Approver + (often) a new procurement review; chain breaks when the business case the deal was sold on can't be demonstrated, when budget is reallocated to a competing priority, or when the original champion has left the company.

**The champion-departure failure mode is one of the most under-measured B2B churn drivers.** A champion who leaves between sale and renewal takes the institutional memory of *why this product was bought* with them. The replacement's evaluation starts from zero; the renewal conversation is effectively a new enterprise sale with the existing product as the incumbent.

Teams that run §3-style information enablement at the pre-sale stage often *also* run it at renewal — the renewal Decision Maker is the same target as the original Decision Maker, just shifted in time. Quarterly business reviews, named-CSM relationships, success-metric dashboards, documented business-case-vs-realized comparisons — all are post-sale-chain instruments analogous to the pre-sale information assets in §3. **Treat onboarding as part of the sale, not as something that begins after the sale ends** — the unit economics of the deal are determined by renewal, and renewal is determined by what happens between contract signature and the first business-case-vs-realized review.
